Sigiriya - Lion's Rock

Explore the history, architecture, and legends of Sri Lanka's most iconic landmark

Historical Background

Sigiriya, also known as Lion's Rock, is one of Sri Lanka's most iconic landmarks and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Located in the central part of the country, it stands 200 meters above the surrounding plains. The rock fortress was built in the 5th century AD by King Kashyapa, who transformed the site into a royal residence and fortress after seizing the throne from his father, King Dhatusena.

The Construction of Sigiriya

King Kashyapa's transformation of the site into a fortified palace was an extraordinary feat of engineering. The Lion's Gate, at the foot of the rock, originally featured a lion-shaped entrance, and the summit was turned into a royal residence with large water tanks and cisterns. The site also features the famous Mirror Wall and stunning frescoes that have captivated visitors for centuries.

The Role of Sigiriya in Sri Lankan Kingdoms

Sigiriya's role in Sri Lankan history intersects with the kingdoms of Anuradhapura and Polonnaruwa. After King Kashyapa's reign ended, the capital moved back to Anuradhapura, and Sigiriya was abandoned. However, it remained a Buddhist monastic site for centuries before being rediscovered in the 19th century by European explorers.

Sigiriya Today

Today, Sigiriya is one of Sri Lanka's most visited and well-preserved historical sites. Its architecture, advanced water management systems, and artistic features like the frescoes make it a testament to ancient Sri Lankan ingenuity and a must-see for tourists from around the world.
